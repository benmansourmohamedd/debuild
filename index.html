About

Mohamed Benmansour is an emerging architectural researcher working at the intersection of housing insecurity, carceral systems, and decolonial building science. His practice does not center on producing buildings, but on documenting how architecture operates as infrastructure — shaping access, control, and survival across institutional and informal environments.

Rather than approaching architecture as a purely design-driven discipline, Benmansour treats spatial conditions as systems embedded within governance, policy, and lived constraint. His work examines shelters, schools, public infrastructure, and informal settlements as sites where architecture quietly enforces or resists broader social structures.

He is the founder of deBUILD (Decolonial Building Science Database), an independent research archive developed outside institutional sponsorship. deBUILD assembles spatial case studies, annotated diagrams, and analytical texts documenting how marginalized communities adapt, repair, and inhabit environments shaped by structural precarity.

Benmansour’s trajectory reflects a hybrid lineage between architectural theory, urban sociology, and public research practices. Influenced by practitioners who build long-term knowledge systems rather than singular design objects, his work prioritizes sustained observation, methodological clarity, and geographic specificity, particularly within the DC–Baltimore region.

Operating early in his career without significant institutional backing, he has focused on developing durable research structures that can outlast credentialing cycles and resist the rapid production norms common to contemporary digital environments. This approach frames architecture as a field of translation — mediating between lived experience, institutional systems, and spatial form.
